### 2c. Voice Register

#### Voice Profile

Stoev Design Group speaks in one voice — first person plural, always — from a single principal whose register is deliberate and architecturally precise. The voice does not rush to the point; it moves through context, lands the insight, and stops.

The default register — project content, planting rationale, site reveals — is controlled and compressed. Instagram captions stay at three sentences maximum. The LinkedIn register extends to a developed paragraph: observation-led, closing with the design implication.

- **Length:** Instagram: 3 sentences maximum. First sentence sets the landscape context, not the program. Delete anything that restates what the image already shows. LinkedIn: one developed paragraph. Open with an observation or question; close with the "so what."
- **Concrete over abstract:** "The oak canopy creates the invitation before any program begins" over "nature provides shade for human connection." Name the plant, the material, the place. Every quality word earns its place only when paired with a named element.
- **Depth over brevity:** Captions carry layered meaning — the image is the surface, the caption adds the design thinking underneath. One-line captions are rarely sufficient.
- **No humor, no levity.** The work is taken seriously; the voice matches. No playful asides, no self-deprecation, no trend-surfing.
- **Pronoun rule:** First person plural only. "We," "our," "us" on brand accounts, from the first meeting through the most recent, without exception. No "I" on brand channels.

### 2d. Voice Boundaries

#### Voice Rules

**Always:**
- First person plural on all brand accounts — no "I," no "you" *(protects: pronoun rule)*
- Name the plant, the material, the place — every quality word paired with a named element *(protects: concrete over abstract)*

- Understated about ecological and philanthropic work — evidence over proclamation, "without sounding pompous" *(protects: no humor/no boosterism)* [Multiple meetings]

**Never:**
- **The landscaper** — "What to plant in Zone 9." "Top drought-tolerant choices for California gardens." Any tip-based, instructional, or zone-specific framing. The prior agency was dismissed for this. "What grows in 9A. Not interested." *(violates: depth over brevity, brand posture)* [Multiple meetings]
- **The theatrical announcer** — "Crowning glory." "Breathtaking." "Stunning reveal." "Transformation." Shilpa's corrections are consistent: remove the drama, trust the image. *(violates: concrete over abstract)* [Multiple meetings]
- **The inaccurate attributor** — "Contemporary architecture" on a traditional home. "Native plants" on a layered salt-tolerant planting. "Coastal" for a project half a mile from water. These are not style choices; they are factual errors with professional consequences. *(violates: factual precision)* [Multiple meetings]
- **The environmental booster** — "Sustainable." "Eco-friendly." "Green design." SDG believes in stewardship and does not market it. "Sustainable without having to tell people we are sustainable." (Patricia, Oct 2025) *(violates: understated ecological stance)* [Oct 2025]
- **The trend reporter** — Anything that reads as "what's popular this season in landscape design." SDG designs for timelessness, not cycles. *(violates: depth over brevity)* [Multiple meetings]
- **"Landscaper" or language that conflates SDG with trade contractors.** A professional distinction with consequences. *(violates: brand posture)* [Multiple meetings]

#### Watch For

- **Theme saturation.** Patricia named this risk in October 2025: "Sustainable without having to tell people we are sustainable." The same principle applies across all four pillars. SDG's ecological commitment, hospitality ambitions, and art-informed design philosophy are all real. The moment these become brand claims rather than evidence, the voice crosses into the boosterism Shilpa explicitly rejects. This is the editorial instinct behind the "understated always" rule — monitor whether a theme is being stated rather than shown.
- **The single-face problem.** Client feedback in Nov 2025 ("I see only Patricia all over LinkedIn") triggered an immediate pivot toward distributed team visibility. Individual staff presence is encouraged; the concern is imbalance. When content planning skews toward one team member, rebalance before posting.
- **Platform register bleed.** "LinkedIn requires a lot of thought. Treating it like Instagram or TikTok is pretty bad." [Shilpa, Mar 2026] The same caption cannot serve both platforms. When a caption reads the same on Instagram and LinkedIn, the LinkedIn version is underdeveloped.

### 3b. Content Tests — Five Gates

| Test | The Question | What It Catches |

| The Swap Test | Could you replace Stoev's name with a competitor's and have the caption still work? If yes, rewrite it. | The most important test. If the name is interchangeable, the brand is invisible. Run on every single post. |

### Pillar Briefs

**The Living Architecture**
Belief: "There's a reason I want this plant at this exact place, because we are building it up to architecture." Plants are structural decisions, not finishing details. The intentionality behind each selection is the point of distinction.
Categories: Completed project images, initial renderings (specify "initial rendering" or "final rendering" — never "this is where every project starts"), planting palette documentation (with Latin names when the palette is distinctive), detail shots of structural plant placement, office design process.
Caption pattern: Open with the landscape context, not the program. The human activity lives inside the landscape, not the other way around. "Nestled under the oak canopy, a shaded space for play" before any mention of bocce. Name the plant. Name the placement decision.
Format and frequency: Carousel for multi-angle project reveals (2–3 per month); single image for planting detail and process shots (1 per week, Instagram).

**Knowledge House**
Belief: "I want LinkedIn to be a knowledge house." [Shilpa, Apr 2026] SDG's LinkedIn presence is about giving, not showcasing.
Categories: Event recaps from ASLA, ULI, and design conferences; ecological and climate research translated for a design audience; industry news with an SDG perspective; framing of what landscape architecture actually does vs. what people assume.
Caption pattern: Open with an observation or question, not a self-promotional announcement. "What the bee crisis has to do with urban design" before "We attended a biodiversity event." Full paragraph — LinkedIn demands thought, not recycled Instagram.
Format and frequency: 2 posts per month, LinkedIn only. Text-forward, image secondary.

**The Traveling Eye**
Belief: Seeing design everywhere is a discipline, not a hobby. Shilpa translates observation directly into form — Ireland's hexagonal stones become a water feature concept, Naoshima's installations inform spatial sequencing.
Categories: Travel photography (Milan, Japan, Ireland, South America), art fair attendance (Art Basel, Salone del Mobile), material and texture close-ups, nursery and seed-sourcing trips, Sonoma test garden updates.
Caption pattern: Name the place. Name what was seen. Connect it to design without over-explaining the connection. "The rhythm of texture is what keeps us all going" [approved, Sep 2025] is the model. LinkedIn version requires a developed paragraph.
Format and frequency: Single image, Instagram-first. 2–3 per month. LinkedIn version is separate, written, not recycled.

**The Site, The Site**
Belief: "It's the site. It's the site. We are very much about the land." The land is always the client.
Categories: Place-specific environmental stories, seasonal site conditions, ecological observations from active projects, the relationship between a project and its surrounding geography. Locations that cannot be named by client can still be named by place.
Caption pattern: Focus on what the land itself does — what it demands, what it gives. Environmental stewardship appears through observation, not proclamation. Stay under 3 sentences on Instagram.
Format and frequency: 2–3 per month, mixed platforms.
